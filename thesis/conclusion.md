# Chapter 6 — Conclusion

## 6.1 Summary of Findings

Experiment 1 evaluated five regression algorithms — RandomForest, Ridge, SVR, GradientBoosting, and HistGradientBoosting — on their ability to predict the Anovator age gap from the full 88-feature biometric set, using 30 repeated GroupShuffleSplit folds grouped by client identity. HistGradientBoosting achieved the lowest mean MAE at 2.43 years (95% CI [2.09, 2.76]), with SVR the weakest at 2.62 years (95% CI [2.30, 2.94]). The remaining three models clustered within 0.04 years of each other. Despite these numerical differences, none of the ten pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests reached the Bonferroni-corrected significance threshold of 0.005, and all ten comparisons must be reported as inconclusive. The finding is scientifically meaningful: the absence of detectable performance differences between a linear model (Ridge) and the best-performing ensemble method suggests that the predictive information in the feature set may be largely linear, or that the 53-individual dataset does not provide sufficient resolution to establish non-linear advantage.

Experiment 2 tested whether augmenting the training set with 1000 GMM-generated synthetic records, pseudo-labelled by the production model, improves prediction quality on real held-out data. Under the RealPlusSynth regime, four of the five models showed statistically significant improvement over RealOnly training, with adjusted p-values ranging from 0.000004 (HistGradientBoosting) to 0.002855 (GradientBoosting) and mean MAE reductions of 0.09 to 0.36 years. Ridge was the exception: its mean MAE under the SynthOnly regime rose to 3.23 years from a baseline of 2.48 years — a 30% degradation explained by the mismatch between the compressed variance of pseudo-labels (standard deviation 1.91 years) and the true label variance (standard deviation 3.30 years). The RealPlusSynth versus SynthOnly comparison was not significant for any model, meaning the real training records could not be shown to add distinguishable value beyond the synthetic corpus alone.

Experiment 3 used HistGradientBoosting to evaluate four cumulative feature groups: the 63-feature anthropometric and metabolic base, plus bioimpedance, plus postural risk scores, plus joint angles (88 features total). Counterintuitively, the base group alone achieved the lowest mean MAE at 2.39 years, with each successive addition of features associated with a marginal increase in error — 0.03 years for bioimpedance, a further 0.002 years for postural risk, and a further 0.006 years for joint angles. The total difference between the 63-feature and 88-feature models was 0.04 years in mean MAE. None of the six pairwise comparisons between groups reached the Bonferroni-corrected threshold of 0.0083, and all are inconclusive. The directional pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that Anovator's scoring formula weights anthropometric and metabolic variables most heavily, though the statistical power available is insufficient to confirm this interpretation.

## 6.2 Answers to the Research Sub-Questions

The first sub-question asked which regression algorithm best approximates the Anovator age gap and whether any performance difference is statistically certifiable. HistGradientBoosting ranks first by mean MAE across 30 folds, but no pairwise difference between models can be claimed as statistically significant at the corrected threshold given the available data. The honest answer is that no algorithm can be recommended over the others on statistical grounds within this dataset, and the choice of HistGradientBoosting for Experiment 3 is justified only by heuristic ranking.

The second sub-question asked whether GMM synthetic augmentation with pseudo-labels improves approximation quality and for which model classes. The answer is yes for ensemble methods and SVR: RealPlusSynth training produces statistically significant MAE reductions for RandomForest, GradientBoosting, HistGradientBoosting, and SVR. The answer is no for Ridge, where pseudo-label variance compression causes substantial degradation under SynthOnly training. Synthetic augmentation through teacher-student pseudo-labelling is therefore not model-agnostic and should not be applied to linear models without adjusting the label distribution to match the real target variance.

The third sub-question asked which biometric feature groups carry independent predictive information relative to the Anovator score. The results provide no statistical evidence that bioimpedance, postural risk, or joint angle features contribute independent predictive signal beyond the anthropometric and metabolic base group. The point estimates directionally suggest the base group alone is sufficient, but all group-pair comparisons are inconclusive at the available sample size. A definitive answer would require either a substantially larger dataset or a finding of significance, neither of which the current study can provide.

## 6.3 Limitations

Four dataset and methodological limitations are documented in full in Section 3.9 of the methodology chapter. In brief: two features use the integer -1 as a sentinel for missing data and are treated as continuous numeric inputs, a misrepresentation that affects all models but cannot be corrected without retraining the production artifact; five features with missingness above 88% become near-constants after imputation and contribute negligible signal; the derived feature aggregated_postural_index is arithmetically redundant within the postural risk group; and the Wilcoxon signed-rank test at Bonferroni-corrected thresholds has limited power with 30 paired observations, which is the primary reason the majority of comparisons across all three experiments are inconclusive rather than resolved. The 53-individual, single-centre dataset is the binding constraint throughout and limits the generalisability of every finding.

## 6.4 Future Work

Several directions are identified as concrete next steps, each building directly on a gap exposed by the current study.

**Dataset expansion** is the highest-priority item. Collecting Anovator scan records from additional health centres would reduce single-gym bias, increase statistical power in all three experiment types, and enable time-based evaluation splits that the current 53-individual corpus cannot support. Targeted recruitment of female participants is also needed, as 81% of current records are male.

**Sentinel value re-encoding** for sportSafeRisk and sportLevel — replacing -1 with NaN followed by indicator-variable encoding and proper imputation — would remove the known feature-space artefact documented in Section 3.9.1 and require a retraining pass on the production model. This is the most contained and highest-impact preprocessing fix available.

**Alternative synthetic generation methods** including CTGAN, TVAE, and Gaussian copula approaches should be evaluated once the real dataset is large enough to train these methods stably (approximately 500 or more records). The current GMM approach was selected on practical grounds; a systematic comparison would establish whether it is optimal or merely sufficient.

**Imputation strategy sensitivity analysis** using KNN or MICE imputation in place of training-fold median imputation would test whether the experiment results are robust to this modelling choice. Given that five features have missingness above 88%, the imputation method could plausibly affect the feature group ablation results.

**Longitudinal evaluation** using time-based train/test splits — training on earlier scans and testing on later ones from the same or different individuals — would test whether the models generalise across time as well as across individuals, a question the current cross-sectional design cannot address.

**Formal power analysis** prior to any dataset expansion effort would quantify the sample size required to detect effect sizes of practical relevance at the Bonferroni-corrected thresholds used here, providing a principled target for future data collection rather than relying on the current incidental sample of 53 individuals.
