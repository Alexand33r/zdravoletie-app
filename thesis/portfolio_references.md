## Part 1.5 — Professionalisation Competency

### Source 1: Science communication to non-technical audiences

Burns, T. W., O'Connor, D. J., & Stocklmayer, S. M. (2003). Science communication: A contemporary definition. *Public Understanding of Science*, 12(2), 183–202. https://doi.org/10.1177/09636625030122004

**Relevance (one sentence):** This paper defines science communication as the use of appropriate skills, media, activities, and dialogue to produce one or more of the following personal responses to science: awareness, enjoyment, interest, opinion-formation, and understanding — providing a conceptual framework that directly underpins the professionalisation challenge of communicating a data science research project to a non-technical company supervisor and a non-specialist clinical audience.

---

### Source 2: Reflective practice in professional development

Schön, D. A. (1983). *The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action*. Basic Books.

**Relevance (one sentence):** Schön's foundational account of reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action — the capacity of practitioners to think critically about their own practice while doing it and after the fact — directly informs the three reflection reports in Section 1.4, particularly the midterm reflection on the project reframing: recognising and correcting a flawed framing in response to expert feedback is a textbook instance of the reflective competence Schön describes.

## Part 2.6 — Learning Objective

### Source 3: Data visualisation and communication of quantitative uncertainty

Spiegelhalter, D., Pearson, M., & Short, I. (2011). Visualizing uncertainty about the future. *Science*, 333(6048), 1393–1400. [VERIFY: page numbers] https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1191181

**Relevance (one sentence):** Spiegelhalter and colleagues analyse how uncertainty in quantitative predictions can and should be communicated to non-specialist audiences, presenting evidence-based recommendations for displaying confidence intervals and probability distributions — directly applicable to the challenge of communicating the model's 95% CI results and 2.43-year MAE to Zdravoletie practitioners who need to act on these outputs without a statistical background.

### Source 4: Professional advisory communication in technical fields

Davenport, T. H., & Patil, D. J. (2012). Data scientist: The sexiest job of the 21st century. *Harvard Business Review*, 90(10), 70–76. [VERIFY: page numbers]

**Relevance (one sentence):** Davenport and Patil describe the data scientist's role as fundamentally advisory — translating analytical findings into organisational decisions by working at the boundary between technical analysis and non-technical stakeholders — and their characterisation of the required communication competencies maps directly onto the learning objective selected for this portfolio: the challenge of making complex model outputs accessible to a non-technical company supervisor and clinical end users.
