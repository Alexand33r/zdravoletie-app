# Plan of Approach

## 1. Project Context and Problem Statement

The Zdravoletie health and rehabilitation centre in Varna, Bulgaria operates an Anovator body scanning system that produces a composite health score called bodyAge — an estimated biological age expressed in years, derived from bioelectrical impedance analysis, postural camera imaging, anthropometric measurement, and a proprietary undisclosed computational formula. Clients and practitioners receive this score without access to the mechanism that generated it. A client informed that their bodyAge exceeds their chronological age by five years has received a clinically weighted statement, but neither the client nor the attending practitioner can determine which measurements contributed most to the result, how large each component's influence was, or which targeted health interventions would meaningfully change the score.

This interpretability gap has direct practical consequences in an advising context. Practitioners at Zdravoletie use bodyAge and its underlying component scores to recommend dietary, exercise, and physiotherapy interventions. Without visibility into the relative weight the Anovator formula assigns to body composition, bioimpedance, postural risk, and joint mobility, practitioners cannot systematically prioritise among competing interventions or explain to clients why the score changed between sessions. The same gap limits the ability to detect potential systematic issues with the scoring formula — for example, whether it assigns disproportionate weight to metrics that are structurally difficult to change, or responds asymmetrically across demographic groups.

A further structural feature of the dataset motivates specific design choices in this study. Analysis of the 158-record corpus assembled for this project reveals a ceiling-effect pattern in the age gap distribution: the signed difference between bodyAge and chronological age has a mean of 0.45 years and a standard deviation of 3.30 years, with the distribution concentrated near zero. This compression limits the variance available for predictive modelling and establishes a practical floor on achievable prediction error — a naive model that predicts zero for every record would achieve a mean absolute error equal to the mean absolute deviation of the distribution. Any trained regressor must consistently and substantially beat this baseline to justify its complexity, and the ceiling effect bears on the interpretation of all reported performance figures.

## 2. Research Question and Framing

The central research question this project addresses is: to what extent can machine learning regression models reverse-engineer the Anovator bodyAge score from the raw biometric inputs the system records, across different algorithm families, training data compositions, and biometric feature group structures?

The framing of this question requires explicit justification, because a superficially similar but fundamentally different problem exists alongside it. Predicting clinical biological age, in the sense studied in the biological age literature, requires a ground truth established against external health endpoints such as mortality follow-up, morbidity incidence, or established biomarkers of physiological ageing. This study has access to no such ground truth, and its research question does not require one. The correct framing is score reverse-engineering: the models in this study learn to approximate an opaque computational formula from its inputs, using the formula's own output as the training target. The target variable used throughout all experiments is the age gap, defined as bodyAge minus chronological age. Modelling this difference rather than bodyAge directly removes the trivially predictable component of chronological age from the learning problem and concentrates modelling effort on the physiological deviation that the Anovator formula computes. A model that achieves low mean absolute error on the age gap is demonstrably approximating the Anovator formula; it makes no claim about whether that formula correctly measures physiological ageing in any clinically validated sense.

## 3. Research Sub-Questions and Experimental Mapping

Three sub-questions operationalise the central research question. Each is addressed by a dedicated experiment whose design was selected because it can answer that specific question given the constraints of the available dataset.

The first sub-question asks which regression algorithm, among five spanning linear, kernel, and ensemble methods, best approximates the Anovator age gap from the full 88-feature biometric set, and whether any observed performance difference can be established as statistically significant. This question is addressed by Experiment 1, a 30-fold repeated cross-validation comparison of five algorithms — Ridge, Support Vector Regression with a radial basis function kernel, RandomForestRegressor, GradientBoostingRegressor, and HistGradientBoostingRegressor — evaluated by mean absolute error, root mean squared error, and coefficient of determination. The 30-fold repeated design is necessary because a single train-test split would yield only a point estimate of performance with high variance from the random partition; repeating the split 30 times yields sufficient paired observations to apply the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

The second sub-question asks whether augmenting the training set with synthetic records generated by a Gaussian Mixture Model and pseudo-labelled by the production model improves approximation quality on real held-out data, and if so, for which model classes. This question is addressed by Experiment 2, which evaluates all five models under three training regimes — real data only, real data augmented with 1000 synthetic records, and synthetic data only — against a consistently real held-out test set. The three-regime structure is essential for isolating the contribution of synthetic distributional coverage from the contribution of additional training examples; a two-condition design could not make this distinction.

The third sub-question asks which biometric feature groups — anthropometric and metabolic measurements, bioimpedance, postural risk scores, and joint angles — carry independent predictive information relative to the Anovator score. This question is addressed by Experiment 3, a cumulative feature group ablation using the best-performing model from Experiment 1. Features are evaluated in four additive increments, so that the marginal predictive contribution of each group can be attributed while holding the preceding groups constant. This cumulative structure was chosen over all-subsets or backward-elimination approaches because the dataset size does not provide sufficient power to resolve the large number of pairwise comparisons those alternatives would require.

## 4. Methodology Overview

Three cross-cutting design decisions apply to all experiments and require justification.

The cross-validation protocol uses GroupShuffleSplit grouped by client identity, repeating 30 times with an 80/20 train-test split of the 53 unique individuals. The group constraint is not optional given the dataset structure. The corpus contains 158 records from 53 individuals — an average of three scan sessions per person — and a random split without the group constraint would routinely assign records from the same individual to both training and test folds. A model trained under those conditions could exploit within-person biometric consistency rather than learning to generalise across individuals, producing performance estimates that reflect how well the model recalls individual profiles rather than how well it handles genuinely unseen clients. Group-level splitting ensures that test performance reflects across-person generalisation, which is the question of practical relevance.

Statistical significance between experimental conditions is assessed using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Bonferroni correction. The Wilcoxon test is appropriate because fold-level error scores cannot be assumed to follow a Gaussian distribution, and because the comparisons are paired across the same cross-validation schedule. The Bonferroni correction is applied in preference to less conservative alternatives because the primary error risk in this setting is a false positive claim that one condition significantly outperforms another — a claim that would misrepresent what the data can certify. A practical consequence of this stringent correction at 30 paired observations is limited statistical power: the majority of comparisons across all three experiments are expected to remain inconclusive, and results that do not reach the corrected threshold are characterised accordingly rather than interpreted as evidence of equivalence.

The synthetic data strategy for Experiment 2 employs a five-component Gaussian Mixture Model fitted to the 158 real records. The GMM was chosen over generative adversarial network or copula-based methods because the 158-record corpus falls below the data scale at which those architectures train reliably. Pseudo-labels for synthetic records are generated by the production model trained on all real data, constituting a teacher-student knowledge distillation setup. The resulting compression of label variance — pseudo-labels have a standard deviation of 1.91 years against the real standard deviation of 3.30 years — is a structural property of the setup that is expected to disadvantage linear models under the SynthOnly regime and is treated as a substantive finding rather than an incidental artefact.

## 5. Project Planning

The project is structured into four phases with a terminal buffer period. Phase 1 addressed technical stabilisation: eleven methodological and implementation bugs in the existing notebook pipeline were identified and corrected, a central configuration module was established, and experimental infrastructure was built. Phase 2 delivered the three core experiments with full statistical testing, saving all result tables and figures to the designated results directory. Phase 3 delivered a multi-page Streamlit dashboard incorporating the surrogate model, SHAP-based feature importance explanations, population comparison visualisations, and PDF report export, deployed to Streamlit Community Cloud. Phase 4 covers thesis writing and competency portfolio assembly, with a hard freeze date of June 22, 2026. An oral defence is scheduled for July 4-5, 2026; the period between the freeze date and the defence is reserved for presentation preparation.

## 6. Known Risks and Mitigations

Four risks material to the study's defensibility were identified before the experiments were run and are documented in the methodology chapter.

The primary risk is insufficient statistical power given the 53-individual corpus. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Bonferroni correction at 30 paired observations has limited power to detect moderate effect sizes at the required corrected thresholds. This risk is mitigated not by additional data collection — which lies outside the project scope — but by consistent characterisation of all inconclusive results as inconclusive, without misrepresenting them as evidence of equivalence.

A second risk is the compression of pseudo-label variance in the synthetic training data for Experiment 2. The production model's regression-to-the-mean behaviour compresses the label distribution of the synthetic corpus relative to real data, creating a systematic mismatch that is expected to disadvantage linear models in the SynthOnly regime. This risk is mitigated by explicit documentation of the mismatch, its mechanistic explanation, and its treatment as a substantive finding about the boundary conditions of the pseudo-labelling strategy.

A third risk is the arithmetic redundancy of the aggregated_postural_index feature within the postural risk group used in Experiment 3. Because the aggregate is the arithmetic mean of six individual postural scores that are themselves present as separate features in the same group, it carries no independent information beyond its components within the group ablation design. This redundancy is documented as a limitation and does not affect the qualitative conclusions of the ablation, but it does inflate the nominal feature count of the postural risk group.

A fourth risk is the use of the integer -1 as a sentinel value encoding missing data for two features — sportSafeRisk and sportLevel — which are passed to all models as continuous numeric inputs. This misrepresents the semantics of absent data and is retained only because correcting it would require retraining the production artifact on which the dashboard depends. It is documented as a limitation and listed as the highest-priority preprocessing correction in future work.
