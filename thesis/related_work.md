# Chapter 2 — Related Work

## 2.1 Biological Age Prediction

The quantification of biological age — an individual's physiological age as distinct from their chronological age — has been an active area of research in gerontology and clinical epidemiology for several decades. Early attempts used simple composite indices of physical performance or blood chemistry, but the foundational theoretical treatment of the problem was provided by Klemera and Doubal (2006), who formalised biological age estimation as a regression problem in which the true biological age is treated as a latent variable estimated from a set of biomarkers. Their method fits a collection of simple linear regressions, one per biomarker, modelling each biomarker as a function of chronological age, and then combines these fits into a composite biological age estimate that is optimal in the sense of minimising the mean squared distance between the estimated biological age and the biomarker values. Klemera and Doubal demonstrated that their method produces lower estimation error than earlier composite approaches and explicitly accounts for the fact that biomarkers have different slopes and residual variances with respect to chronological age. This formulation established the key conceptual move that underlies all subsequent biological age research: chronological age is treated as informative but imperfect proxy for a latent physiological quantity, and the goal is to estimate that latent quantity more accurately from measurable inputs.

The field advanced substantially with the development of epigenetic clocks, which exploit the observation that DNA methylation at specific CpG sites in the genome changes in a highly age-predictable manner across tissue types. Horvath (2013) demonstrated that a penalised regression model trained on methylation data from 51 tissue types could predict chronological age with a median absolute error of approximately 3.6 years and that systematic deviations from the predicted age — termed age acceleration — were associated with disease status and mortality risk. Building on this foundation, Levine and colleagues (2018) introduced PhenoAge, a composite biological age measure derived from nine clinical blood biomarkers — including albumin, creatinine, glucose, C-reactive protein, lymphocyte percentage, mean corpuscular volume, red blood cell distribution width, alkaline phosphatase, and white blood cell count — combined with chronological age through a Gompertz proportional hazards model trained on mortality data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey. The defining feature of PhenoAge is that it is calibrated against a mortality endpoint: a PhenoAge that exceeds chronological age is not merely a label but carries a validated association with elevated all-cause mortality risk. Lu and colleagues (2019) extended this line of work with GrimAge, an epigenetic clock trained on plasma protein levels and smoking pack-years via DNA methylation proxies, optimised to predict time-to-death. GrimAge demonstrated substantially stronger associations with lifespan and health outcomes than earlier methylation clocks and has become a reference standard for biological ageing research in cohort studies.

The trajectory from Klemera-Doubal through PhenoAge to GrimAge illustrates two defining characteristics of the biological age prediction literature that are relevant for positioning the present work. First, all three methods are validated against external clinical endpoints — mortality, disease incidence, or functional decline — that are independent of the scoring method itself. The ground truth is not the output of a proprietary algorithm but a population-level health outcome that has been measured longitudinally in thousands or tens of thousands of individuals. Second, the biomarker sets used — blood chemistry, DNA methylation, plasma protein levels — are obtained through clinical laboratory procedures under standardised conditions.

The present project differs from this literature in both respects. The ground truth is not a clinical endpoint but the proprietary bodyAge score computed by the Anovator scanning platform, whose formula is unpublished and not externally validated against mortality or morbidity data. The inputs are biometric measurements obtained from a non-clinical scanning device in a gym and wellness centre context: impedance bioelectrical analysis, postural camera imaging, and manual anthropometric measurements. The research question is accordingly not whether a model can predict how quickly an individual will age in a clinical sense, but whether a model can learn to reproduce the Anovator formula's output from its inputs — a reverse-engineering problem. This framing is not a limitation relative to the biological age literature; it is a different research question with different scientific content. The contribution of this project is an analysis of the approximability of a proprietary health score and the informational structure of its input features, not a claim about biological ageing.

## 2.2 Synthetic Data Generation in Small Medical Datasets

The scarcity of labelled data is a recurring constraint in medical machine learning, where data collection is costly, privacy regulations limit sharing, and cohort sizes at individual institutions are often in the hundreds rather than the thousands. A range of methods has been developed to augment small datasets with synthetic records that preserve the distributional characteristics of the original data without reproducing any real individual's record.

The earliest and most widely used approach in the context of class imbalance is the Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique introduced by Chawla and colleagues (2002), which generates new minority-class instances by interpolating between existing instances in feature space. SMOTE operates in a purely local neighbourhood context and produces synthetic records that are convex combinations of real records, which limits its ability to generate samples in regions of the input space that the original data does not cover, and it does not model the global joint distribution of features.

Generative approaches that model the full joint distribution offer a more principled alternative. Gaussian Mixture Models fit a weighted sum of multivariate Gaussian distributions to the observed data and generate synthetic samples by drawing from the fitted mixture. GMMs are computationally efficient, produce closed-form likelihood estimates, and require relatively few observations to fit stably. The number of components and the covariance structure can be selected by information criteria such as the Bayesian Information Criterion, and the resulting model is directly interpretable as a soft partition of the input space into ellipsoidal clusters. These properties make GMMs particularly well suited to small tabular datasets in the low-hundreds-of-records regime.

Generative adversarial networks represent the current frontier of synthetic data generation for tabular medical data. Xu and colleagues (2019) introduced CTGAN, a Conditional Generative Adversarial Network specifically designed for tabular data that addresses two problems endemic to applying standard GANs in this context: the multi-modal non-Gaussian distribution of continuous variables, which CTGAN handles through mode-specific normalisation, and the class imbalance problem in categorical variables, which it addresses through conditional generation. CTGAN has demonstrated strong results on several tabular benchmark datasets and has been adopted in the synthetic health data literature. However, GAN training is known to be sensitive to the ratio of training samples to model parameters, and mode collapse — in which the generator learns to produce a narrow subset of the training distribution rather than its full diversity — is a documented failure mode that becomes substantially more likely when training sample sizes fall below approximately one thousand records per class. With 158 records and 88 features, stable CTGAN training would require careful architectural tuning and regularisation that lies outside the scope of this project.

Copula-based approaches, as systematised in the Synthetic Data Vault framework by Patki and colleagues (2016), model the joint distribution of variables by separately estimating each variable's marginal distribution and then using a copula to capture the dependence structure. Gaussian copulas assume a multivariate Gaussian dependence structure after marginal transformation and can be fitted efficiently on small datasets. More flexible vine copulas allow for asymmetric and tail dependencies but require more data to estimate reliably. The Synthetic Data Vault approach is well suited to datasets where the dependency structure is the primary concern and marginal distributions can be reasonably approximated.

For this project, a five-component Gaussian Mixture Model with full covariance matrices was selected as the synthesis method on the grounds of suitability to the dataset scale and computational tractability. With 158 records and 88 features, a five-component GMM is parameterised by five mean vectors, five covariance matrices, and five mixture weights — a total of approximately 39,000 parameters, which is modest relative to the number of training samples when regularisation is applied. The GMM was fitted on the real dataset in the Data_Gen.ipynb notebook and used to generate 1000 synthetic records. CTGAN was excluded because the training corpus of 158 records falls below the regime in which GAN training is reliably stable without extensive hyperparameter search. Copula approaches were excluded as out of scope for the current experimental programme. The GMM's limitation — its assumption that the data distribution can be approximated by a mixture of Gaussians, which may not accurately capture heavy-tailed or highly non-linear feature dependencies — is acknowledged as a structural constraint on the synthetic augmentation experiment. The experiment in Chapter 4 evaluates empirically whether GMM-derived synthetic records, when used as a teacher-student augmentation corpus, improve prediction quality on real held-out data, rather than assuming that synthetic record quality is sufficient.

## 2.3 Feature Importance and Interpretability in Health Machine Learning

The deployment of machine learning models in health-related contexts introduces an obligation of interpretability that is distinct from the pure predictive performance requirements of general machine learning applications. Clinicians, regulators, and institutional review processes require not only that a model predicts accurately but that the basis for its predictions can be articulated and audited. This requirement has driven the development of post-hoc explanation methods that decompose model predictions into feature-level contributions.

The SHapley Additive exPlanations framework, introduced by Lundberg and Lee (2017), provides a theoretically grounded approach to this decomposition. SHAP values are derived from cooperative game theory: the contribution of each feature to a particular prediction is computed as the average marginal contribution of that feature across all possible orderings in which features are added to the model, analogous to the Shapley value that allocates the payoff of a cooperative game fairly among its players. This foundation gives SHAP values three properties that competing attribution methods do not simultaneously satisfy: local accuracy (the sum of SHAP values for all features equals the model output minus the expected output), consistency (if a model changes so that a feature's marginal contribution increases, its SHAP value cannot decrease), and missingness (features that are absent contribute zero). These properties make SHAP values uniquely well suited for comparing feature attributions across different models and different data instances.

Lundberg and colleagues (2020) developed TreeSHAP, an algorithm that exploits the structure of tree-based ensemble models to compute exact SHAP values in polynomial time, removing the exponential complexity of the naive Shapley calculation. TreeSHAP makes SHAP analysis computationally feasible for random forests and gradient boosting models with hundreds of estimators and tens of features, and it has been widely adopted in health machine learning applications. In a landmark clinical application, Lundberg and colleagues (2018) used SHAP explanations to identify the features driving a gradient boosting model's predictions of sepsis onset in intensive care patients, demonstrating that SHAP could surface clinically meaningful feature importance patterns that were subsequently validated by domain experts and used to guide treatment protocols.

In the context of the present project, SHAP analysis serves two purposes. Globally, summary plots of mean absolute SHAP values across the full dataset provide an empirical ranking of feature importance that complements the ablation experiment in Experiment 3: whereas the ablation evaluates feature groups by their effect on held-out prediction error, SHAP values quantify the contribution of each feature to the model's predictions on the training distribution. Locally, force plots decompose individual predictions into feature contributions, providing the interpretable patient-level output that is displayed in the Zdravoletie Streamlit dashboard to support clinical discussion. The combination of group-level ablation (Experiment 3) and individual SHAP attribution (dashboard) provides a two-level interpretability layer: the group ablation addresses which categories of biometric measurement matter for approximating the Anovator score, while the individual SHAP values explain why a particular client received a particular age gap prediction.

It should be noted that SHAP values explain the model's behaviour, not the underlying physiological process. In this study, the model being explained is an approximation of the Anovator formula, not a validated biological age model. The SHAP values therefore characterise the information structure of the Anovator score as recovered by the model, subject to the approximation error and the feature encoding limitations described in the methodology chapter. This distinction is important for interpreting any clinical insights derived from SHAP outputs: they are insights into what Anovator's formula appears to weight, not into independent physiological truth.
