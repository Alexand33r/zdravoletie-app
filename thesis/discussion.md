# Chapter 5 — Discussion

## 5.1 The Null Result in Experiment 1 Is a Finding, Not a Failure

The absence of statistical significance across all ten pairwise model comparisons in Experiment 1 is the most important single result in this thesis and it must be stated precisely. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test on 30 paired fold observations, corrected for ten simultaneous comparisons at a threshold of alpha = 0.005, found no evidence that any two of the five models differ in their ability to approximate the Anovator bodyAge score. The adjusted p-value for the closest comparison — SVR versus HistGradientBoosting — was 0.043, an order of magnitude above the corrected threshold.

This result does not mean the models are equivalent. Claiming equivalence from a non-significant Wilcoxon test would be to commit the fundamental error of accepting the null hypothesis, which the signed-rank test is not designed to license. What the result does mean is that the study, as constituted, cannot resolve the performance difference between these models with the required statistical confidence. This distinction matters for the oral defence: the claim being made is not that Ridge performs as well as HistGradientBoosting, but that the available data do not permit a definitive ranking. The confidence intervals reported in Table 4.1 span approximately 0.65 to 0.78 years — a range that is large relative to the mean differences between models, which are all below 0.20 years.

The finding has scientific content precisely because it is unexpected. The literature on machine learning model comparison routinely shows that ensemble methods such as gradient boosting outperform linear regression and kernel SVMs on tabular biological data. The fact that Ridge regression, which fits a single hyperplane in a high-dimensional space with L2 regularisation, achieves mean MAE within 0.05 years of HistGradientBoosting suggests one of two things: either the predictive information in these 88 features is largely captured by linear combinations of those features, implying that Anovator's proprietary formula may itself be approximately linear in the inputs; or the test folds are too small — averaging 12 records drawn from 11 individuals — to discriminate non-linear signal from noise in a dataset of this size. Both interpretations are scientifically meaningful and are discussed further in the context of the feature group results below.

The practical implication is equally clear. For any operational deployment of a model intended to approximate the Anovator score within this data context, the choice among these five algorithms does not matter in a statistically defensible sense. The selection of HistGradientBoosting as the model for Experiment 3 is justified by its numerical rank at the top of the MAE ordering, but this selection carries an explicit caveat that it represents a heuristic choice rather than a certified one.

## 5.2 Synthetic Augmentation Helps Ensemble Methods and Damages Ridge

Experiment 2 produces the strongest statistical results in the study. For HistGradientBoosting, the RealOnly versus RealPlusSynth comparison yields an adjusted p-value below 0.000004, the most significant result obtained in any test across the three experiments. For GradientBoosting, RandomForest, and SVR, the same comparison produces adjusted p-values below 0.003. The mean MAE reduction under RealPlusSynth compared to RealOnly ranges from 0.09 years (Ridge) to 0.36 years (HistGradientBoosting). These are not trivial improvements: a 0.36-year reduction in mean MAE relative to a baseline of 2.43 years represents a 15% improvement in the primary error metric.

The mechanism is interpretable within the teacher-student framework. The production model was trained on all 158 real records and captures the full distributional structure of the Anovator score. Its predictions on 1000 GMM-generated synthetic records provide the student models with a much larger effective training set (158 plus 1000 records under RealPlusSynth, 1000 records under SynthOnly), even if the information content of those additional records is derived from the same 158 real observations. Tree-based ensemble methods and kernel SVMs benefit from larger training sets because they rely on local density estimation or partitioning that becomes more reliable as the coverage of the input space increases. The synthetic records fill gaps in the biometric input space that the 53 individuals in the real dataset leave sparsely covered.

Ridge regression is the exception. Under the SynthOnly regime, Ridge's mean MAE rises to 3.23 years from a RealOnly baseline of 2.48 years — a 30% degradation — and the associated confidence interval widens dramatically to [2.14, 4.31]. This behaviour is a direct consequence of the pseudo-label distribution. The production model's predictions on the synthetic records have a standard deviation of 1.91 years, compared to 3.30 years in the real label distribution. Linear models fit their coefficients to minimise squared error across the training distribution; when the training distribution artificially compresses the label variance, the fitted linear model learns a relationship that is calibrated to a narrower output range than the true target. When this model is then evaluated on real test folds where age_gap ranges more widely, the mismatch between training label variance and test label variance produces systematically biased predictions. Tree-based and kernel methods are less susceptible to this effect because they make fewer assumptions about the global shape of the target distribution and are more responsive to local input-output structure.

This finding has a practical implication: synthetic augmentation through teacher-student pseudo-labelling is not model-agnostic. Before applying it in an operational context, the distributional alignment between real and pseudo-label variance should be verified, and linear models should either be excluded from synthetic training or trained on a label distribution that has been re-scaled to match the real target variance.

The RealPlusSynth versus SynthOnly comparison is not significant for any model, meaning that the real training records cannot be shown to add distinguishable value over the synthetic corpus alone. This is a nuanced finding. It does not mean the real records are redundant. Under RealOnly, all five models achieve lower MAE than the pseudo-labels' intrinsic noise floor suggests should be achievable from teacher predictions alone, which implies that the true labels in the 158 records carry information the teacher has not fully extracted. Rather, the failure to detect a significant difference between RealPlusSynth and SynthOnly reflects the limited power available to detect what is likely a small marginal contribution of approximately 100 to 130 real records against an 1000-record synthetic corpus.

## 5.3 Feature Group Saturation and What It Implies About Anovator's Formula

The most counterintuitive finding in the study is that the 63-feature anthropometric and metabolic group alone achieves lower mean MAE (2.39 years) than any larger feature set, including the full 88-feature model (2.43 years). The monotonic increase in mean MAE as features are added is small — the G1-to-G4 difference is 0.04 years — and all six pairwise comparisons are statistically inconclusive. Nevertheless, the directional pattern is unambiguous in the data: none of the three additional feature groups (bioimpedance, postural risk, joint angles) contributes a detectably positive marginal effect, and the point estimates suggest a slight negative marginal effect.

Several interpretations are consistent with this result. The first, and most important for the thesis framing, concerns the structure of the Anovator formula itself. Anovator's bodyAge computation is proprietary and unpublished. If the formula places low or negligible weight on bioimpedance raw values, postural camera risk scores, and joint angles in favour of anthropometric and metabolic measurements, then a model attempting to reverse-engineer the formula would find no additional predictive information in those feature groups beyond what the base group already captures. The feature group ablation results are consistent with this hypothesis: the Anovator score may be predominantly determined by the body composition, demographic, and metabolic variables that constitute Group 1, with the more specialised measurements serving as supplementary or diagnostic output rather than primary scoring inputs.

The second interpretation concerns the training set size. With approximately 145 real records per training fold, adding 10, 20, or 25 features to a 63-feature model introduces a modest increase in the curse of dimensionality that a tree-based algorithm must navigate. HistGradientBoosting builds its trees using gradient boosting with early stopping implicitly, which provides some protection against overfitting, but the added feature dimensions still increase the number of candidate splits evaluated at each node and may dilute signal with noise in the biometric measurements that show limited variance across the 53 individuals in the dataset.

The third interpretation concerns data quality in the added feature groups. The bioimpedance values (r20 and r100 measurements) depend on electrode placement and individual hydration status at the time of scanning, introducing measurement noise. The postural risk scores are categorical or ordinal outputs of a computer vision algorithm applied to static posture images and may be less reliable across sessions for the same individual. The joint angles are similarly image-derived estimates. If these measurements carry meaningful signal for the Anovator score, that signal may be buried under measurement noise at the scale of 158 records.

Whichever interpretation is correct, the operational implication is consistent: at this data scale, there is no statistical justification for preferring the full 88-feature model over the 63-feature anthropometric and metabolic subset. If the dataset were expanded to several hundred or several thousand individuals, a repeat of this experiment would carry sufficient power to detect whether the additional feature groups contribute marginal predictive value. Until then, the finding must be reported as inconclusive on the direction of the true marginal effect, and as consistent with the hypothesis that Anovator's scoring places primary weight on anthropometric and metabolic variables.

## 5.4 SHAP Feature Importance: Physiological Interpretation

Global SHAP values computed via TreeExplainer across all 158 records reveal a pronounced concentration of predictive weight in a small number of anthropometric and metabolic features, with bioimpedance and postural risk measurements contributing substantially less. The analysis ranks features by their mean absolute SHAP value — the average magnitude of each feature's contribution to the predicted age gap, irrespective of direction.

Waist circumference (wc) is the single dominant predictor by a large margin, with a mean absolute SHAP value of 0.69 years, more than twice that of any other feature. Waist circumference is an established marker of central adiposity and visceral fat accumulation, both of which are strongly associated with cardiometabolic risk and accelerated physiological ageing. Its dominance in the surrogate model is consistent with its likely role as a primary input to the Anovator bodyAge formula: central obesity is the biometric signal most directly linked to the health outcomes that commercial biological age scores typically aim to capture.

The second and third most influential features — aerobicGoal (mean |SHAP| = 0.31) and agility (0.18) — warrant a caveat before physiological interpretation. AerobicGoal is an Anovator-computed training recommendation, not a raw biometric measurement: the platform derives it from the same underlying data that produces bodyAge. Its high SHAP rank suggests the surrogate model has partially learned to use Anovator's own intermediate outputs as predictors of the final score, which is expected in a reverse-engineering setting but limits the physiological interpretability of this feature's contribution specifically. Agility, by contrast, is a direct fitness assessment result; lower agility scores correspond to poorer physical performance and, through the model's learned associations, to higher predicted age gaps.

Intra-abdominal fat (inFat, mean |SHAP| = 0.16) and BMI (0.15) follow as the fourth and sixth most important features respectively. Visceral fat accumulation is mechanistically linked to insulin resistance, chronic inflammation, and accelerated cellular ageing, making its prominence in the model physiologically coherent. The first bioimpedance feature to appear in the ranking is r20LeftLeg — raw impedance at 20 kHz in the left leg — at rank five (mean |SHAP| = 0.15). Its positive mean SHAP value (higher impedance associated with higher age gap) may reflect lower tissue hydration or reduced muscle density in the lower limb, though the interpretation of raw impedance values at a single frequency is not straightforward without segmental body composition context. The first joint angle feature — front_right_leg_angle — appears at rank eleven (mean |SHAP| = 0.05), well below the anthropometric and metabolic cluster.

The pattern of global SHAP importances provides feature-level corroboration for the Experiment 3 finding. The eight highest-ranked features by mean absolute SHAP are all members of the anthropometric and metabolic group (Group 1). The first Group 2 feature (bioimpedance) appears at rank five with less than one-quarter of the contribution of waist circumference, and the first Group 4 feature (joint angle) ranks eleventh. This gradient directly supports the interpretation in Section 5.3: the Anovator formula appears to draw its predictive structure primarily from body composition, fitness, and metabolic measurements rather than from impedance spectra, postural camera outputs, or joint angle estimates.

One feature in the top ten merits explicit flagging: sportLevel (mean |SHAP| = 0.075, rank nine) carries the sentinel encoding problem described in Section 5.5.1, with -1 present in 70% of records. Its SHAP prominence may partly reflect the model learning to distinguish the sentinel subpopulation from the measured subpopulation rather than capturing the true ordinal relationship of sport level with biological ageing. Its contribution should therefore be treated as an encoding artefact rather than a physiologically meaningful signal until the sentinel values are re-encoded properly.

## 5.5 The Underpowered Wilcoxon Caveat and Its Scope

The statistical power limitation identified in Section 3.9.4 of the methodology applies differently across the three experiments and must be characterised with precision rather than applied as a blanket disclaimer.

In Experiment 1, the power limitation is binding. All ten adjusted p-values are well above the corrected threshold, and the closest raw p-value (0.0043 for SVR versus HistGradientBoosting) is already near the corrected threshold before adjustment. The 30-fold sample of paired differences has insufficient variance to reject the null for these comparisons. Increasing N_CV_SPLITS from 30 to 50 would narrow the 95% confidence intervals by approximately 18%, which could allow the SVR-versus-HistGradientBoosting comparison to reach significance if the true effect size is close to what the point estimates suggest. That said, the 0.19-year mean MAE difference between these two models, compared to a fold-level standard deviation of approximately 0.87 years, implies a very modest effect size, and even a 50-split protocol may not be sufficient.

In Experiment 2, the power limitation is not binding for the five significant comparisons, which reach adjusted p-values as low as 0.000004. The effects detected there are large enough to be identified even under the relatively conservative test setup. The limitation is relevant for the non-significant regime comparisons, particularly Ridge's RealOnly versus RealPlusSynth comparison (raw p = 0.64) and the universally non-significant RealPlusSynth versus SynthOnly comparisons, where small effects may simply be below the resolution of the current test.

In Experiment 3, the power limitation is clearly binding. The lowest raw p-value observed is 0.21, for the G1-versus-G4 comparison — a value that would not reach even the uncorrected 0.05 threshold. This suggests either that the true effect of adding bioimpedance, postural risk, and joint angle features to the base group is genuinely negligible on the Anovator approximation task, or that the effect exists but is smaller than the 30-fold signed-rank test can detect at this sample size. The two hypotheses cannot be distinguished without either a larger dataset or a power analysis conducted prior to data collection. As neither was available, the correct reporting posture is to state that the experiment is consistent with the null hypothesis of no marginal contribution from the additional feature groups, while acknowledging that genuine small effects cannot be excluded.

None of these observations are apologetic qualifications of a study that failed to find what it was looking for. Underpowered tests that return non-significant results are honest results. The obligation is to report them accurately and to not overstate what the available evidence supports.

## 5.6 Known Limitations and Their Bearing on Interpretation

### 5.6.1 Sentinel Values in sportSafeRisk and sportLevel

The treatment of -1 as a numeric feature value for sportSafeRisk (present in 37% of records) and sportLevel (present in 70% of records) introduces a systematic misrepresentation of the feature space. For tree-based models, the value -1 functions as a splitting criterion that separates records where the measurement was computed from those where it was not, which may incidentally capture some genuine structure — but the boundary at -1 has no biological meaning and conflates absence-of-measurement with a sub-zero risk score. For Ridge, the signed value -1 directly enters a linear function of features, assigning these records a large negative contribution from a coefficient trained on a mixture of true scores and sentinels. The fact that the Experiment 1 results place Ridge within 0.05 MAE of HistGradientBoosting despite this encoding problem may indicate either that sportLevel and sportSafeRisk carry low overall weight in the Anovator formula (consistent with the interpretation in Section 5.3 and the SHAP evidence in Section 5.4), or that the tree-based models also do not benefit sufficiently from proper encoding of these features at this sample size to register a detectably different result.

The correct resolution — replacing -1 with NaN followed by indicator-variable encoding and then imputation — is left for future work because it would require retraining the production model and redeploying the Streamlit dashboard. The experiments as conducted are internally consistent: all three experiments treat these features identically, and the comparison between models (Experiment 1) and between regimes (Experiment 2) is unaffected by the sentinel encoding because all conditions share the same encoding. What the sentinel encoding prevents is a clean interpretation of the absolute MAE values as reflecting the best achievable approximation of the Anovator score; a properly encoded version of these features could yield lower error, but that cannot be determined from the present experiments.

### 5.6.2 Near-Constant Features After Imputation

The five features with missingness above 88% — leftVision, rightVision, bloodMaxPressure, bloodMinPressure, and restingHeartRate — contribute negligible variance after training-fold median imputation and therefore function as near-zero-weight features in all trained models. Their presence does not distort the experimental comparisons because they are treated identically across all conditions. However, they inflate the nominal dimensionality of the feature groups. In Experiment 3, the anthropometric and metabolic group nominally contains 63 features, but five of these carry essentially no independent information. The effective feature count for Group 1 is closer to 58. This reduces the gap in effective information between Group 1 and the full 88-feature model (Group 4) from 25 features to approximately 20, which slightly weakens the already modest basis for expecting G4 to outperform G1.

### 5.6.3 Redundant Aggregate in the Postural Risk Group

The inclusion of aggregated_postural_index alongside its six component features (frontHeadRisk, humpbackRisk, kneeRisk, pelvisRisk, postureRisk, spineRisk) in the postural risk group means that Group 3 and Group 4 contain a feature that is an exact deterministic function of other features already present in the same group. For tree-based models, this redundancy has no effect on the learned function — the algorithm can select the aggregate or its components as equivalent split candidates and will produce the same tree structure regardless of which it chooses. For Ridge, collinearity between the aggregate and its components would ordinarily introduce numerical instability in coefficient estimation, but L2 regularisation mitigates this by penalising the magnitude of correlated coefficient pairs. The practical consequence of the redundancy is therefore small, but the presence of a deterministically derived feature among the postural risk group variables means that the nominal count of ten postural risk features overstates the independent information content of that group by one feature.

### 5.6.4 Dataset Scale and External Validity

Fifty-three unique individuals drawn from a single health centre during a two-year collection window is a small and geographically concentrated sample. The Anovator client base at Zdravoletie may not be representative of the broader population that the Anovator system is deployed to serve, and the age gap distribution observed here (mean 0.45 years, standard deviation 3.30 years) may differ from the distribution across other centres or national populations. The experiments are therefore best characterised as an analysis of the reverse-engineering problem within this specific dataset, not as a generalisation claim about model suitability for Anovator score prediction at population scale. Extending the dataset to include records from multiple Anovator-equipped centres, across a wider age range, and with more balanced representation across sex and fitness level, would be the minimum requirement for a generalisation claim.

## 5.7 Synthesis

Taken together, the three experiments produce a coherent picture. The five models are statistically indistinguishable on the real-data-only approximation task (Experiment 1), which, combined with the feature group saturation finding (Experiment 3), suggests that the predictive information available in the 88 features is largely captured by their linear structure and by the anthropometric and metabolic subset in particular. Synthetic augmentation through teacher-student pseudo-labelling produces statistically significant improvements for ensemble methods and SVR (Experiment 2), indicating that the 53-individual training corpus is the primary constraint on approximation quality rather than model class or feature richness. Ridge's failure under the SynthOnly regime provides a clear case study in the conditions under which synthetic augmentation is contraindicated. The consistent absence of statistical power across the significance tests is not a methodological weakness but a transparent report of what a 53-individual dataset can and cannot support. These findings together constitute an honest characterisation of the limits and opportunities of reverse-engineering a proprietary health score at this data scale.
